\begin{abstract}
We introduce the \emph{ORC Transition Graph}~(OTG), a deterministic directed graph over local optima derived from Ollivier--Ricci curvature~(ORC), as a structural analysis tool for discrete fitness landscapes.
Each local optimum has exactly one outgoing edge to the optimum reached by following its most-negative-curvature direction and hill-climbing, requiring only a local $O(k^2)$ computation.
We study OTG on NK landscapes, epistatic W-model benchmarks ($N\!=\!16$, full enumeration), random MAX-SAT ($N$ up to 100), QAP (permutation swaps, $n$ up to 9), and TSP (2-opt, $n$ up to 50 via sampling).
The OTG extracts cross-basin information invisible to standard local optima networks~(LONs): using only 1-hop neighborhood data, it achieves up to $9.2\times$ better attractor quality than LON-d1 at equal information cost on epistatic W-model landscapes, and even outperforms the stochastic 3-hop LON-d3 on those instances.
A structural crossover in OTG topology (from acyclic to cyclic at moderate epistasis) correlates with when ORC-guided perturbation outperforms random perturbation in iterated local search~(ILS).
The OTG framework applies to any graph with the disjoint-neighborhood property (Proposition~1), which we prove covers binary hypercubes, permutation swap graphs, \emph{and} TSP 2-opt neighborhoods, which constitute the three foundational neighborhoods in combinatorial optimization.
ORC's per-step escape advantage grows monotonically with neighborhood size (from $3.1\times$ to $18.3\times$ over random on TSP, $n\!=\!8$ to $50$), although its computational cost of $O(k^2)$ per optimum limits algorithmic use to settings where fitness evaluations are expensive.
Per-optimum ORC metrics converge from ${\sim}20$ sampled optima ($<$1\% error), while full OTG structural analysis remains limited to enumerable landscapes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ===================================================================

Fitness landscape analysis~(FLA) provides the theoretical foundation for understanding why metaheuristic optimizers succeed or fail on specific problem instances~\cite{pitzer2012comprehensive} and for automated algorithm selection~\cite{kerschke2019automated}.
Classical metrics---fitness-distance correlation~(FDC)~\cite{jones1995fitness}, autocorrelation length~\cite{stadler1996landscapes}, information content~\cite{vassilev2000information}, and exploratory landscape analysis~(ELA) features~\cite{mersmann2011exploratory}---characterize global statistical properties such as ruggedness and smoothness.
Local optima networks~(LONs)~\cite{ochoa2008first,ochoa2017understanding} go further by representing basin connectivity as a graph whose edges are discovered through stochastic perturbation followed by hill climbing~(HC).

Both approaches share a limitation: classical metrics provide scalar summaries blind to local geometric structure, while LONs provide connectivity but not \emph{directional} information---at a local optimum, which specific neighbor escapes the basin?
LON construction also depends on perturbation distance $d$ and requires many stochastic trials per optimum.

We address these limitations through Ollivier--Ricci curvature~(ORC)~\cite{ollivier2009ricci}, a discrete analogue of Ricci curvature that measures how neighborhood distributions of adjacent nodes diverge or converge.
Negative curvature identifies directions where the landscape geometry ``spreads apart''---the signature of inter-basin saddle regions.
From ORC, we construct the \emph{ORC Transition Graph}~(OTG): a deterministic directed graph where each local optimum points to the optimum reached by following its min-ORC direction.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item We formalize fitness-lifted ORC for discrete optimization landscapes and prove that negative curvature is guaranteed at every local optimum on graphs with the disjoint-neighborhood property, covering binary hypercubes, permutation swap graphs, and TSP 2-opt neighborhoods.
\item We introduce the OTG, a deterministic, parameter-free directed graph over local optima derived from optimal-transport-based curvature.
On epistatic binary landscapes, the OTG achieves up to $9.2\times$ better attractor quality than LON-d1 at equal information cost; across binary, permutation, and tour search spaces, ORC's per-step escape advantage grows monotonically with neighborhood size.
\item We validate the framework algorithmically through ORC-guided iterated local search~(ILS) and show that OTG structural properties---specifically the presence or absence of cycles---indicate when ORC-guided perturbation adds value over random perturbation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ===================================================================

A \emph{fitness landscape} $(S, \mathcal{N}, f)$ consists of solutions $S$, neighborhood function $\mathcal{N}\!: S \to 2^S$, and fitness $f\!: S \to \mathbb{R}$~\cite{stadler1996landscapes}.
A solution $x^*$ is a \emph{local optimum} (minimization) if $f(x^*) \le f(y)$ for all $y \in \mathcal{N}(x^*)$.
LONs~\cite{ochoa2008first,ochoa2016local} compress the landscape into a graph whose nodes are local optima and whose edges encode transitions discovered by perturbing a solution (flipping $d$ random bits) and hill-climbing.
LON features---including funnel structure~\cite{ochoa2016funnels}, fractal dimension~\cite{thomson2022fractal}, and phase-transition indicators~\cite{ochoa2020maxsat}---predict search difficulty~\cite{ochoa2017understanding}, but edge construction requires $T$ stochastic trials per optimum (typically $T\!\ge\!50$) and the resulting funnel decomposition depends on~$d$.

Ollivier~\cite{ollivier2009ricci} defined Ricci curvature for metric-measure spaces; Lin et al.~\cite{lin2011ricci} gave a modified graph formulation.
For a graph edge $(x, y)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:orc}
\kappa(x, y) = 1 - \frac{W_1(\mu_x, \mu_y)}{d(x, y)}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_x, \mu_y$ are neighborhood probability measures and $W_1$ is the Wasserstein-1 distance~\cite{villani2009optimal}.
Negative curvature signals diverging neighborhoods---a signature of community bridges~\cite{ni2019community} and GNN bottlenecks~\cite{topping2022understanding,sandhu2015graph}; positive curvature signals contraction.
We choose Ollivier's formulation over Forman's~\cite{samal2018comparative}: on triangle-free graphs (including all three neighborhoods in Corollary~\ref{cor:search_spaces}), Forman curvature is the constant $4\!-\!2k$ for every edge on unweighted $k$-regular graphs, and depends only on endpoint-local quantities even with fitness weighting---missing the distributional matching that gives ORC its cross-basin signal.
To our knowledge, ORC has not been applied to fitness landscape analysis.

\section{ORC on Fitness Landscapes}
\label{sec:method}
% ===================================================================

\subsection{Fitness-Lifted ORC}

We define ORC on the search graph $(S, \mathcal{N})$ with uniform measures $\mu_x$ over $\{x\} \cup \mathcal{N}(x)$.
The transport cost between $a \in \mathrm{supp}(\mu_x)$ and $b \in \mathrm{supp}(\mu_y)$ is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lifted}
c(a, b) = \delta(a, b) + \gamma \cdot |f(a) - f(b)|
\end{equation}
where $\gamma \ge 0$ controls fitness sensitivity and the overlap indicator is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:overlap}
\delta(a, b) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } a = b \\ 1 & \text{if } a \text{ or } b \in \mathrm{supp}(\mu_x) \cap \mathrm{supp}(\mu_y) \\ 2 & \text{otherwise (both exclusive)} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
This is a conservative coarse-graining of graph distance computable from 2-hop information alone, preserving $O(k^2)$ locality.
For uniform measures of equal support size, $W_1$ reduces to an optimal assignment problem solved by the Hungarian algorithm in $O(k^3)$~\cite{kuhn1955hungarian}; the fine-grained complexity of this reduction has been formally established~\cite{dasgupta2023computing}.

\textbf{Parameter sensitivity.}
We use $\gamma\!=\!1$ throughout.
Importantly, the ORC-guided \emph{direction} (Algorithm~\ref{alg:escape}) is $\gamma$-invariant: changing $\gamma$ scales all fitness differences uniformly, so the optimal transport matching order---and thus the min-ORC neighbor---does not change.
Empirically, escape rates are identical to three decimal places across $\gamma \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$ on all 480 instances; only absolute curvature values change.
The framework is thus parameter-free for navigation; $\gamma$ matters only for difficulty prediction, where $\gamma\!=\!1$ balances structural and fitness terms.
This invariance is a consequence of the \emph{linear} fitness term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:lifted}: a nonlinear cost $g(|f(a)-f(b)|)$ would break it, though such extensions are a natural direction for future work.

\subsection{Why ORC Sees What 1-Hop Methods Cannot}

\begin{proposition}[ORC Cross-Neighborhood Decomposition]
\label{prop:decomposition}
Let $G = (V,E)$ be a regular graph of degree $k$ in which adjacent nodes share no common neighbors: $\mathcal{N}(x) \cap \mathcal{N}(y) = \emptyset$ for all $(x,y) \in E$.
Then $|\mathrm{supp}(\mu_{x^*}) \cap \mathrm{supp}(\mu_y)| = 2$ (only $x^*$ and $y$ are shared), and the optimal transport cost decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
(k\!+\!1)\,W_1(\mu_{x^*}, \mu_y) = C_{\mathrm{shared}} + \min_{\sigma} \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} \bigl[2 + \gamma\,|f(a_i) - f(b_{\sigma(i)})|\bigr]
\end{equation}
where $C_{\mathrm{shared}} \le 2 + \gamma(\Delta + \Delta')$ accounts for the two shared elements, $\{a_i\} = \mathcal{N}(x^*) \setminus \{y\}$, $\{b_j\} = \mathcal{N}(y) \setminus \{x^*\}$ are the $k\!-\!1$ exclusive neighbors on each side, and $\sigma$ ranges over permutations of $\{1,\ldots,k\!-\!1\}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $\mathcal{N}(x^*) \cap \mathcal{N}(y) = \emptyset$, the only shared support elements are $x^*$ and $y$ themselves.
Any assignment $\pi: \mathrm{supp}(\mu_{x^*}) \to \mathrm{supp}(\mu_y)$ partitions into: (i)~the two shared positions ($x^*, y$) contributing $C_{\mathrm{shared}}$, and (ii)~the $k\!-\!1$ exclusive pairs, each incurring $\delta = 2$ plus a fitness-difference term.
The optimal $\sigma$ minimizes the total fitness-comparison cost.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Applicability to Standard Search Spaces]
\label{cor:search_spaces}
Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition} applies to:
\textbf{(i)}~the $N$-dimensional binary hypercube with bit-flip neighborhood ($k = N$),
\textbf{(ii)}~the symmetric group $S_n$ with pairwise-swap neighborhood ($k = \binom{n}{2}$), and
\textbf{(iii)}~Hamiltonian tours with 2-opt neighborhood ($k = n(n\!-\!3)/2$).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{(i)} On the hypercube, flipping bit $i$ in $x$ and bit $j$ in $y = x \oplus e_p$ yields the same neighbor only if $e_i = e_p \oplus e_j$; since single bit-flips cannot equal a two-bit change, $\mathcal{N}(x) \cap \mathcal{N}(y) = \emptyset$.
\textbf{(ii)} On $S_n$, if $y = x \circ \tau_{ab}$, a common neighbor $z$ requires $\tau_{ij} = \tau_{ab} \circ \tau_{kl}$ for transpositions $\tau_{ij}$, $\tau_{kl}$.
The product of two distinct transpositions is a 3-cycle or double transposition---never a transposition.
\textbf{(iii)} A 2-opt move is a segment reversal $\rho_{p,q}$ mapping position $i \mapsto p\!+\!q\!+\!1\!-\!i$ for $i \in \{p\!+\!1,\dots,q\}$ and fixing all others---an affine map with \emph{slope $-1$} on its support.
A common neighbor requires $\rho_{a,b} = \rho_{c,d} \circ \rho_{p,q}$ for distinct non-trivial reversals.
\emph{Disjoint supports:} the composition has non-contiguous support (two separated intervals with a fixed gap), impossible for a single reversal.
\emph{One nested in the other} (WLOG $\mathrm{supp}(\rho_{p,q}) \subset \mathrm{supp}(\rho_{c,d})$): on the inner interval, $(\rho_{c,d} \circ \rho_{p,q})(i) = i + \delta$ is a \emph{translation} (slope $+1$), since each reversal contributes slope $-1$ and $(-1)(-1) = +1$.
Any single reversal has slope $-1$ on its entire support, so the composition cannot be one.
\emph{Partial overlap:} within the overlap the composition transitions from slope $+1$ (where both act) to slope $-1$ (where only one acts)---again incompatible with a single reversal.
Verified computationally at $n \le 8$ (all 25{,}200 edges) and by sampling at $n \le 11$ (all $>$200{,}000 tested edges have overlap 0).
\end{proof}

These three search spaces---binary strings (SAT, NK), permutations with swaps (QAP, scheduling), and tours with 2-opt (TSP)---cover the foundational neighborhoods in combinatorial optimization.

The exclusive-pair term is the key: it computes an optimal matching between the fitness distributions visible from $x^*$ and from~$y$.
At a basin boundary, the two sides' fitness distributions diverge, making curvature strongly negative.
By contrast, LON-d1 evaluates each neighbor $y$ using only $f(y)$ and the downstream hill-climbing destination, discarding the $(k\!-\!1)$ exclusive neighbors entirely---precisely the distributional comparison that ORC captures.

\subsection{Negative Curvature Guarantee}
\label{sec:theory}

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:negative}
Let $x^*$ be a strict local optimum on any degree-$k$ graph satisfying the disjoint-neighborhood condition of Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition} ($k \ge 4$), with cost~\eqref{eq:lifted}--\eqref{eq:overlap} and $\gamma > 0$.
If neighbor $y$ satisfies $\Delta = f(y) - f(x^*) < (k\!-\!3)/((k\!+\!1)\gamma)$, then $\kappa(x^*,y) < 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
From Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition}, every assignment incurs exclusive-pair structural cost at least $2(k\!-\!1)$, giving $W_1 \ge 2(k\!-\!1)/(k\!+\!1)$.
With edge cost $c(x^*,y) = 1 + \gamma\Delta$:
$\kappa(x^*,y) \le 1 - 2(k-1)/[(k+1)(1+\gamma\Delta)]$,
which is negative iff $\Delta < (k-3)/((k+1)\gamma)$.
\end{proof}

For the binary hypercube with $k = N = 16$, $\gamma = 1$: the bound requires $\Delta < 0.76$.
For QAP with $n = 8$ ($k = 28$): $\Delta < 0.86$.
Across all 5{,}000+ local optima in our binary benchmarks and 1{,}500+ QAP optima, \textbf{100\% satisfy the condition}.
The min-ORC neighbor $y^* = \arg\min_y \kappa(x^*, y)$ identifies the direction of maximal neighborhood divergence---the strongest saddle direction (Alg.~\ref{alg:escape}).

\subsection{ORC-Guided Escape and the OTG}
\label{sec:otg_def}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{ORC-Guided Escape from a Local Optimum}
\label{alg:escape}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Local optimum $x^*$, fitness $f$, neighborhood $\mathcal{N}$, parameter $\gamma$
\FOR{each $y \in \mathcal{N}(x^*)$}
  \STATE Compute $\kappa(x^*, y)$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:orc}--\eqref{eq:overlap}
\ENDFOR
\STATE $y^* \leftarrow \arg\min_{y} \kappa(x^*, y)$
\RETURN \textsc{HillClimb}$(y^*)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Applying Algorithm~\ref{alg:escape} at every local optimum of a landscape defines the \emph{ORC Transition Graph}~(OTG): a deterministic directed graph where each optimum has exactly one outgoing edge to the optimum reached by following its min-ORC neighbor and hill-climbing.
The OTG is a functional graph; its terminal attractors are either self-loops (sinks) or directed cycles.
The \emph{funnel} of an attractor is the set of all optima whose OTG path converges to it.
Unlike LONs~\cite{tomassini2022lon}, the OTG is deterministic and parameter-free: it requires no perturbation distance or stochastic trials.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ===================================================================

\subsection{Benchmark Problems}
\label{sec:benchmarks}

We evaluate on five benchmark families spanning three search spaces:

\textbf{NK landscapes}~\cite{kauffman1993origins} define tunably rugged fitness via $K$~epistatic interactions per bit on length-$N$ binary strings.
We test $K \in \{4, 8, 12\}$ at $N\!=\!16$ (30 instances each, full enumeration).

\textbf{W-model}~\cite{weise2018wmodel} applies neutrality, epistasis, and ruggedness transformations controlled by a difficulty parameter $\nu$ to a base OneMax problem.
We test $\nu \in \{3, 4, 6\}$ at $N\!=\!16$ (30 instances each, full enumeration).

\textbf{Random MAX-SAT.}
Random 3-SAT instances with $N \in \{20, 50, 100\}$ variables and clause-to-variable ratio $\alpha \in \{3.0, \ldots, 6.0\}$ (20 instances each).
Local optima are collected via random-restart HC (500--2{,}000 restarts).
This domain has well-studied phase transitions~\cite{ochoa2020maxsat} and no structural overlap with NK or W-model.

\textbf{Random QAP.}
Quadratic assignment problems with uniform random flow\slash distance matrices and pairwise-swap neighborhood, $n \in \{6, 7, 8, 9\}$ (20~instances each, full enumeration).

\textbf{Random TSP.}
Random-matrix TSP instances with 2-opt neighborhood.
Full enumeration at $n \in \{8, 9, 10\}$ (20 each, 4--7 optima per instance); sampling-based analysis at $n \in \{15, 20, 30, 50\}$ (20 each, 300 random-restart 2-opt runs per instance).
TSP instances at small $n$ serve to validate the theoretical framework on an exact ground truth; sampling-based runs at larger $n$ test scaling.

The five families were chosen to cover three distinct search spaces (binary strings, permutations, tours), a range of landscape ruggedness levels, and both enumerable and sampling-only regimes.

\subsection{Algorithms and Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}

\textbf{Hill Climbing~(HC).}
Best-improvement (steepest descent) HC: at each step, all $k$ neighbors are evaluated and the one with the best fitness is selected.
HC terminates when no neighbor improves on the current solution, yielding a local optimum.
Ties are broken uniformly at random.

\textbf{Random-Restart Hill Climbing~(RR-HC).}
Repeat HC from uniformly random initial solutions until the budget of fitness evaluations~(FEs) is exhausted.
Return the best local optimum found.

\textbf{Iterated Local Search~(ILS)}~\cite{lourenco2003ils}.
From a local optimum, apply a perturbation (bit-flips) and re-run HC; repeat until the FE budget is exhausted.
We test four ILS variants, all using a fixed perturbation strength of 3~bit-flips for fair comparison:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Rand-ILS}: flip 3 random bits.
\item \textbf{ORC+Pert}: flip the min-ORC bit (Algorithm~\ref{alg:escape}) plus 2 random bits.
\item \textbf{ORC-ILS}: flip only the min-ORC bit (1 bit; no random component).
\item \textbf{MG-ILS}: flip only the MinGap bit (1 bit; see below).
\end{itemize}
ORC-ILS and MG-ILS use only 1-bit perturbation; they are included as ablations to isolate the effect of the directed bit.

\textbf{MinGap.}
The neighbor $y$ with the smallest fitness difference $|f(y) - f(x^*)|$ (argmin-$|\Delta|$ heuristic).
This greedy baseline uses only fitness values, not distributional information.

\textbf{LON-d1.}
At a local optimum $x^*$, the LON-d1 destination is the mode HC outcome over all $k$ single-bit-flip neighbors (1-hop, deterministic).

\textbf{LON-d3.}
At a local optimum $x^*$, the LON-d3 destination is the mode HC outcome from 100 random 3-bit perturbations (3-hop, stochastic).

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\textbf{Escape rate~(\%).}
Fraction of local optima where a method reaches a strictly better optimum after one perturbation + HC step.
This is the primary metric shared across all experiments.

\textbf{Mean terminal rank.}
Normalized fitness rank of the attractor reached by following OTG/LON paths (0.0 = best, 0.5 = random).

\textbf{Top-5\% attractor~(\%).}
Fraction of optima whose OTG/LON path terminates at an attractor in the top 5\% by fitness.

\textbf{Compression.}
$\#$funnels~$/$~$\#$optima---lower means better funnel aggregation.

\textbf{Success rate~(\%).}
Fraction of ILS runs finding the global optimum within the FE budget (used for the ILS comparison in Section~\ref{sec:ils}).

\textbf{Computational cost.}
At $N\!=\!16$, ORC costs ${\sim}$120~FEs (${\sim}$3\,ms); at $N\!=\!100$, ${\sim}$5\,000~FEs (${\sim}$18\,ms).
At $N\!=\!100$ on MAX-SAT (10\,s wall-clock budget), Rand-ILS completes $25\times$ more iterations and finds better solutions; ORC's $3.7\times$ per-step advantage cannot overcome the iteration deficit when evaluations cost ${\sim}$0.001\,ms.
ORC-guided search becomes cost-effective when individual evaluations cost ${\ge}\,1$\,ms (e.g., simulation-based optimization).

\subsection{Convergence Under Sampling}
\label{sec:convergence}

On W-model at $N\!=\!16$, subsampling $R$ optima uniformly (20 subsamples $\times$ 10 instances), per-optimum metrics converge fast: escape rate within 2\% error at $R\!=\!5$, $<$1\% at $R\!=\!20$.
Structural metrics converge slowly---at 50\% coverage, attractor rank is still $3.4\times$ worse than ground truth, because the OTG cannot route to unsampled attractors.
Per-optimum ORC metrics are thus reliable from ${\sim}20$ restarts at any scale; full OTG structural analysis remains limited to enumerable landscapes.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The strongest OTG results (up to $9.2\times$ attractor quality improvement over LON-d1) come from full enumeration at $N\!=\!16$ on W-model---a scale where exhaustive analysis is feasible but not representative of real-world problem sizes.
At $N\!=\!100$ on MAX-SAT, Rand-ILS outperforms ORC+Pert in wall-clock time when evaluations are cheap ($\ll$1\,ms).
On QAP, ORC's advantage over MinGap is marginal ($1.06\times$) on random instances; structured QAPLIB instances may behave differently.
For neighborhoods \emph{without} the disjoint-neighborhood property (e.g., 3-opt, LK moves), Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition}'s decomposition does not apply and ORC requires the full $O(k^3)$ Hungarian algorithm; characterizing ORC's behavior on such graphs is an open question.
A full comparison with the flacco/ELA ecosystem~\cite{mersmann2011exploratory} in an algorithm selection pipeline remains future work.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ===================================================================

The ORC Transition Graph demonstrates that optimal transport over neighborhood fitness distributions captures cross-basin structure invisible to 1-hop methods, with the attractor quality gap over LON-d1 reaching up to $9.2\times$ on epistatic W-model landscapes at $N\!=\!16$.
The disjoint-neighborhood property (Corollary~\ref{cor:search_spaces}) gives the framework a unified foundation across the three foundational neighborhoods in combinatorial optimization: bit-flips, permutation swaps, and TSP 2-opt.
ORC's per-step escape advantage grows monotonically with neighborhood size ($3.1\times$ to $18.3\times$ over random on TSP from $n\!=\!8$ to $n\!=\!50$; analogous trends on binary and swap spaces), though translating this into algorithmic gains remains cost-dependent.
OTG structural properties---particularly the acyclic-to-cyclic crossover---correlate with when ORC-guided perturbation adds value in ILS, providing practitioners with a diagnostic signal.
Per-optimum metrics converge from ${\sim}20$ samples, making ORC a practical addition to the FLA toolkit.
